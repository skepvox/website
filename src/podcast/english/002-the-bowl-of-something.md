## Learning Guide

The sections below collect the vocabulary, usage notes and practical context.

## Vocabulary

**confidence**: Confidence is the feeling that something is going well, or that a person can handle a situation. Grace uses it for a smell, which is not literal. The kitchen smells bold, active, and slightly too sure of itself. Example: Grace asks why the kitchen smells like confidence and panic.

**panic**: Panic is sudden fear or rushed anxiety. In a kitchen, panic can mean too many pans, too many smells, and not enough control. Grace pairs panic with confidence because Sam's cooking looks both energetic and unstable.

**a wooden spoon**: A wooden spoon is a cooking spoon made of wood. It is common in home kitchens, especially for stirring soup, sauce, or pasta. Here it is standing upright, as if the kitchen has become dramatic.

**upright**: Upright means standing straight up, not lying down or leaning. The spoon is upright in the pan, which makes it look almost like a witness. Example: the wooden spoon is standing upright as if it has seen things.

**soup**: Soup is a liquid dish, usually warm, made with vegetables, meat, beans, or other ingredients. It can be light and simple, but Sam's soup becomes crowded with garlic, carrots, paprika, and pasta.

**more or less**: More or less means basically, almost, or near enough. It works best for a whole state: more or less ready, more or less the same, more or less agreed. Sam says "more or less soup" because the pan has reached the general category of soup, although even he sounds unsure. For a time or quantity, "around seven" or "about two cups" is usually clearer.

**a neighbor**: A neighbor is someone who lives near you, often in the same building or street. Mrs Patel is the neighbor who invited Grace and Sam downstairs. The word also carries a social duty: you try to be helpful without making the visit awkward.

**garlic**: Garlic is a strong-smelling ingredient used in cooking. A little can be warm and rich; too much can take over the room. Sam says the garlic "got ideas", making it sound as if the ingredient became ambitious.

**sevenish**: Sevenish means around seven o'clock, not exactly seven. The ending "-ish" makes the time relaxed and approximate. It is useful for informal plans, but it would be too loose for a train ticket or an official appointment.

**soup-adjacent**: Adjacent means next to or very near something. "Soup-adjacent" is comic because it treats a social offering as something near soup, not quite simply soup. It helps Sam avoid saying that he may have overdone the plan.

**a glug**: A glug is an informal amount of liquid poured from a bottle. It is not measured exactly. In cooking talk, "a glug of oil" means pour a small, reasonable amount, using judgment rather than a measuring spoon.

**a bit**: A bit means a small amount. "A bit of onion" is casual and approximate. It works in a home kitchen because the exact quantity does not always matter, but it still needs common sense.

**a couple of**: A couple of usually means two, or sometimes a small number close to two in casual speech. "A couple of carrots or so" layers two softeners, so "two" quietly becomes "two, maybe more." Grace notices that Sam may be letting the quantities drift too far.

**smoked paprika**: Smoked paprika is a red spice made from dried peppers with a smoky flavor. Grace recognizes it immediately. Sam calls it "the red powder thingy", which shows that he is cooking without the exact word.

**thingy**: Thingy is a very informal placeholder for an object whose name you do not know, cannot remember, or do not need to specify. It is fine between flatmates. It would sound unserious in formal instructions.

**a pan**: A pan is a container used for cooking on a stove. Sam says the pan "developed a position on garlic", as if the pan itself had an opinion. The phrase makes the cooking sound more dramatic than controlled.

**pasta**: Pasta is food made from wheat dough, often served with sauce or in soup. Grace is surprised to see it because Mrs Patel had already made soup. Sam added pasta as "texture" and "reassurance".

**a container**: A container is something that can hold food or objects. A bowl can be a container. Grace finally understands that Mrs Patel needed an empty bowl, not a bowl of something in a separate pan.

**napkins**: Napkins are pieces of cloth or paper used at a meal to clean hands or mouth. Grace lists bowls, spoons, maybe napkins, and "stuff like that" to show the correct practical category.

**support soup**: Support soup is Sam's comic name for the extra soup he accidentally made. It sounds like a helpful companion, but Grace decides it should stay upstairs. The phrase turns the mistake into a character.

## Expressions and Other Uses

**"More or less"**

"More or less" means basically, almost, or near enough, but it usually describes a whole situation, not one exact number. Someone might say, "The room is more or less ready", "We're more or less agreed", or "The two versions are more or less the same." The phrase answers a question like "Has it reached the general state?" rather than "What is the exact value?"

That is why "more or less soup" is funny and useful here. Sam is not saying how many bowls of soup he made. He is saying the pan has become something that belongs roughly in the soup category. There may be doubts. There may be pasta. Still, from his point of view, it is soup enough to defend.

The phrase can sound natural when the speaker is rounding off a state. It sounds less natural if used for a precise time or quantity. "I will arrive around seven" or "sevenish" is smoother than "I will arrive more or less at seven." In this scene, Sam uses "more or less" well, but the thing he is describing is already suspicious.

**"Or something"**

"Or something" is a relaxed way to leave a category open. A person might say, "We could get coffee or something", "Bring a jacket or something", or "Maybe write a note or something." The speaker gives one example and lets the listener understand similar options. The sound matters. Light and open, it feels collaborative. Flat and bored, it can sound careless.

Mrs Patel's message says, "Bring a bowl or something. I've made soup." The intended meaning is practical: bring a bowl, a container, a spoon, napkins, or something in that family. Sam hears the words too creatively. He treats "a bowl or something" as if it meant a bowl full of something. Grace repairs the category with "stuff like that", another informal list extender. Her phrase is vague too, but it is useful because the category is clear.

This contrast is the heart of the scene. Vagueness is not a problem by itself. It works when both people share the category. It fails when one person hears too much freedom inside the soft edge.

## Cultural and Practical Note

In many apartment buildings, neighborly invitations are informal. A message may say "come down around seven", "bring a bowl or something", or "maybe bring a spoon". The words are not meant to sound legal or exact. They create a relaxed social space: the food is ready, the visit is friendly, and nobody wants to sound like they are issuing instructions.

That relaxed style depends on shared practical understanding. If someone says, "Bring a bowl or something" after saying they have made soup, the usual expectation is that you bring a container for serving or eating. The word "something" does not open the door to any possible contribution. It points to a family of similar objects. This is why Grace immediately names bowls, spoons, napkins, and "stuff like that". She hears the category, not just the words.

Cooking talk also uses approximate quantities naturally. "A glug of oil", "a bit of onion", and "a couple of carrots or so" sound normal when the cook already understands the dish. They are less helpful when the cook is guessing. Approximate language is efficient when the task is familiar; it becomes risky when the speaker uses it to cover uncertainty.

The social tone matters as much as the vocabulary. Sam is not trying to be difficult. He wants to be helpful, and his vague language lets him keep that helpful self-image for a little too long. Grace is direct, but she is not cruel. She corrects the practical mistake, keeps the visit alive, and gives the extra soup a harmless role: it can stay upstairs and think about its choices.

The useful pattern is simple. Soft, approximate words make everyday talk warmer and less stiff. But they still need a shared frame. "Sevenish" works because both people know the visit is informal. "A glug" works if the cook knows roughly what the dish needs. "Or something" works if the listener knows what kinds of things belong in the group. Without that frame, vagueness stops being relaxed and starts becoming extra work for the person listening. The warmer the relationship, the lighter the wording can be.

The opposite is also useful to hear. If you tell a plumber there is "a sort of dripping thingy" under the sink, or tell a doctor a pain has lasted "a week or so", the other person will probably ask for precision. In friendly kitchen talk, the soft edge can help. In practical service talk, the same softness may delay the answer.
